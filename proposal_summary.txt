PROPOSAL SUMMARY — CMSC 678 Project
Do Modern Deep Learning Architectures Disproportionately Benefit BCI-Inefficient Users?
Chris Dollo & Madeline Rippin
================================================================================

WHAT IS THIS ABOUT?
A brain-computer interface (BCI) lets people control devices — like a prosthetic arm or
wheelchair — using only their brain signals, recorded by EEG (electrodes on the scalp).
The problem: 15–30% of users simply can't operate a BCI well, even after extensive
training. This is called BCI inefficiency, and it hits hardest for the people who need
BCIs most — stroke patients, ALS patients, amputees. Most research just reports an
average accuracy across all users and moves on. We want to ask: do newer, fancier
models actually help the users who are struggling, or do they just make the easy users
even better?

RESEARCH QUESTIONS
1. Does EEGNet (a small CNN) help low-performing subjects more than the traditional
   method (FBCSP), relative to the baseline? Prior work showed this for 2-class tasks —
   we're testing it on the harder 4-class version.
2. Does Mamba (a newer state-space model) push that effect even further than EEGNet?
3. Does data augmentation (adding noise, sliding windows) close the gap between
   struggling and strong users, or does it just lift everyone equally?

WHAT WE'RE DOING
We run three models on the BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a (9 subjects, 4 motor imagery
classes: left hand, right hand, feet, tongue, 288 trials each):

  - FBCSP + LDA     — the traditional signal processing baseline (won the original 2008
                      competition)
  - EEGNet          — compact CNN, our deep learning baseline
  - Mamba (MI-Mamba) — a newer state-space model that handles long sequences efficiently

We split subjects into "high performers" and "low performers" using a median split on
FBCSP accuracy. Then for each model we measure how much it improves over FBCSP for each
group. If the improvement is bigger for low performers, that model is more equitable.
We also repeat the whole analysis after augmenting the training data (3x Gaussian noise
copies + sliding window segmentation) to see if more data helps the harder subjects.

HOW THIS FITS INTO PRIOR WORK
- The FBCSP baseline replicates Ang et al. (2012), the original competition winner.
- EEGNet comes from Lawhern et al. (2018), the standard compact CNN benchmark.
- A 2022 PLoS ONE paper showed CNNs help inefficient users more than CSP+LDA — but only
  for 2-class tasks with online BCI accuracy scores. We extend that to 4-class offline.
- Mamba for EEG is brand new (MI-Mamba, 2025; STMambaNet, 2024) — nobody has looked at
  whether it specifically helps struggling subjects.
- Data augmentation (noise + sliding window) has been tried on this dataset before but
  never analyzed through the lens of who benefits most.

ANTICIPATED CHALLENGES
- Only 9 subjects total, so our high/low groups are just 5 and 4 people. Statistical
  power is limited — we'll frame results as exploratory, not definitive.
- Mamba requires CUDA-specific code that may be tricky to install. If it fails we have
  a fallback implementation (STMambaNet).
- BCI IV-2a has no online accuracy scores, so we use FBCSP kappa as a proxy for "BCI
  literacy." We'll validate this against known per-subject rankings in published papers.

ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
The core motivation is fairness: BCI systems currently perform worst for the users who
need them most. If we find that certain architectures or augmentation strategies
disproportionately help low performers, that has real clinical implications for which
models get deployed in assistive technology contexts. The dataset itself was collected
from healthy volunteers, so there's no direct participant harm, but any findings would
need validation on actual patient populations before clinical use — healthy subjects and
patients with motor impairments have meaningfully different EEG. We'll flag this gap
explicitly. Better algorithms alone aren't enough; accessible BCI hardware and software
design matters too.
